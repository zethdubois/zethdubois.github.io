My response:

In SDT lingo, sensitivity increases as the distance between the noise and signal curves increases. Stated more practically, the overlap of signal and noise area decreases as sensitivity increases. An increase in sensitivity will therefor reduce the intersection of noise and signal, making the distinction easier to detect.

Biomedical screenings use a different definition for sensitivity and an inversely proportional term, specificity. These are effectively opposite ends of the criterion continuum, where “sensitive” equates to a SDT liberal criterion [β < 1], and “specific” equates to a conservative criterion [β > 1]

Discussion on ββ and d′d′

_...from_ [a source for SDT](http://wise.cgu.edu/wise-tutorials/tutorial-signal-detection-theory/signal-detection-d-defined-2/)

The most commonly used SDT measure of sensitivity is d’ (d prime), which is the standardized difference between the means of the Signal Present and Signal Absent distributions. To calculate d’, we need to know only a person’s hit rate and false alarm rate. The formula for d’ is as follows:

d′=z(FA)–z(H)d′=z(FA)–z(H)
where FA and H are the False Alarm and Hit rates, respectively, that correspond to right-tail probabilities on the normal distribution. Thus, z(FA) and z(H) are the z-scores that correspond to these right-tail p-values represented by FA and H. Larger absolute values of d’ mean that a person is more sensitive to the difference between the Signal Present and Signal Absent distributions. d’ values near zero indicate chance performance.

_...from_ [another friendly blog](http://elvers.us/perception/sdtGraphic/) One of the most important features of signal detection theory is that the values of d' and β are independent of each other. That is, any (reasonable) value of d' can be paired with any (reasonable) value of β. Thus, **bottom-up processes (as measured by d') and top-down processes (as measured by β) are independent of each other** as they should be.

In terms of medical screening terminology, an increase in specificity, an a decrease in sensitivity would result in fewer false alarms, but a increase in legitmante misses.

One measure to be considered is the resolution/overall accuracy of the screening process. Let us imagine that there are a series of screenings, sorting first with high sensitivity, then resampling positive cases with higher specificity to catch false positives. In this way two or more methods can be combined to increase the cumulative sensitivty [sdt], optimizing for cost by using coarser, likely less expensive, methods. Finally, there must be some value in assessing the state of screening art, equipment and methods. It's conceivable that clinics are using systems in need of updating, or training refreshers for technicians.

Impartially speaking, the optimal beta must be evaluated with respects to the cost of misses (a risk of fatality) and the cost of undergoing unnesseary treatment.
